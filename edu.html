1. Motion

This chapter introduces the concept of motion, explaining how objects change their position over time and how this change is measured and described.

  • Types of Motion:
    • Linear Motion: Movement along a straight line.
    • Circular Motion: Movement along a circular path.
    • Periodic Motion: Repeated motion in equal time intervals.
  • Distance and Displacement:

    Distance: The total path covered by an object. It is a scalar quantity.
    Displacement: The shortest path between the initial and final positions of the object. It is a vector quantity.

  • Speed and Velocity:

    Speed: The rate of change of distance. It is scalar.
    Velocity: The rate of change of displacement. It is vector.
    Average and instantaneous speed/velocity are also discussed.

  • Acceleration:

    The rate of change of velocity with respect to time. It can be positive (speeding up) or negative (slowing down).

  • Graphical Representation of Motion:
    • Distance-Time Graph: Shows the motion of an object over time.
    • Velocity-Time Graph: Helps calculate acceleration and displacement.
  • Equations of Motion:

    These equations relate initial velocity (\(u\)), final velocity (\(v\)), acceleration (\(a\)), time (\(t\)), and displacement (\(s\)):

    • \(v = u + at\)
    • \(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
    • \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)
  • Uniform Circular Motion:

    Motion in a circular path with constant speed but changing velocity due to direction changes.

2. Gravitation

The chapter on Gravitation discusses the universal force of attraction between two masses and its implications in our daily lives and the cosmos. It explains the concepts of gravity, gravitational force, and how they govern the motion of celestial and terrestrial objects.

  • Universal Law of Gravitation:

    This law states that every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

    Formula: \( F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \)

    • F: Gravitational force
    • G: Gravitational constant (\(6.674 \times 10^{-11} \, Nm^2/kg^2\))
    • m1, m2: Masses of the two objects
    • r: Distance between the centers of the two objects
  • Free Fall:

    An object under the influence of gravity alone experiences a free fall. During free fall, all objects, irrespective of their masses, accelerate at the same rate due to gravity.

    Acceleration due to gravity: \( g = 9.8 \, m/s^2 \)

  • Mass and Weight:
    • Mass: The amount of matter in an object. It is constant and measured in kilograms.
    • Weight: The force with which an object is attracted towards the center of the Earth. It varies depending on the gravitational force and is measured in newtons.

      • Formula: \( W = m \cdot g \)

  • Thrust and Pressure:
    • Thrust: The force exerted by a body perpendicular to a surface.
    • Pressure: Force per unit area. \( P = \frac{F}{A} \)
    • Pressure is measured in Pascals (Pa).
  • Archimedes' Principle:

    This principle states that a body submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by it.

    • Used in designing ships and submarines.
    • Explains why some objects float while others sink.
  • Relative Density:

    The ratio of the density of a substance to the density of water.

    Formula: \( \text{Relative Density} = \frac{\text{Density of Substance}}{\text{Density of Water}} \)

    It has no unit as it is a ratio.

3. Work, Power, and Energy

This chapter explores the concepts of work, power, and energy, which are fundamental in understanding physical processes.

1. Work

- Definition: Work is done when a force is applied to an object and the object moves in the direction of the force.
- Formula: W = FScos(Thita), where F is the force, d is the displacement, and ? is the angle between the force and displacement.
- Unit: Joule (J).
- Conditions: Work is only done if there is displacement and a component of force in the direction of displacement.
- Positive and Negative Work: Work can be positive (force and displacement in the same direction) or negative (force and displacement in opposite directions).

2. Power

- Definition: Power is the rate at which work is done.
- Formula: P = W/t, where W is work and t is time.
- Unit: Watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.
- Types of Power: Instantaneous and average power.

3. Energy

- Definition: Energy is the capacity to do work.
- Types of Energy: Kinetic energy (energy of motion) and potential energy (energy of position).
- Kinetic Energy Formula: KE = 1/2 mv�, where m is mass and v is velocity.
- Potential Energy Formula: PE = mgh, where m is mass, g is acceleration due to gravity, and h is height.
- Unit: Joule (J).

4. Work-Energy Theorem

The work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy: W = ?KE.

5. Conservation of Energy

Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another.
Example: A pendulum converts potential energy to kinetic energy and vice versa.
